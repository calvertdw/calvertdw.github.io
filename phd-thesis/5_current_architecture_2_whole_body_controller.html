A System for Fast, Resilient, and Adaptable Loco-Manipulation Behaviors on Humanoid Robots

Whole-Body Controller

The behavior coordination layer interfaces with a whole-body and walking controller that executes arbitrary trajectory requests and footsteps. The behavior runtime sends requests to the controller and monitors execution through status messages.

We use the whole-body momentum-based control framework developed by Koolen et al. 31. The general structure and control flow of the whole-body controller and walking implementation are illustrated in 1 and 2. It is a model-based torque-control scheme centered on a quadratic program (QP) that reconciles motion tasks expressed as constraints on the joint-acceleration vector, with QP constraints for foot-ground contact and force-limited grasping. The action primitives use joint-space and spatial task-space objectives provided by this controller.

The QP 31 solves for the desired joint-acceleration vector (\dot{v_d}) and basis-vector multiplier (\rho), minimizing momentum error subject to the motion-task set, equations of motion, and contact friction:

$$ \begin{aligned} \underset{\dot{v}_{\text{d}}, \rho}{\text{minimize}} \quad & (A\dot{v}_{\text{d}} - b)^T C_h (A\dot{v}_{\text{d}} - b) + \rho^T C_\rho \rho + \dot{v}_{\text{d}}^T C_v \dot{v}_{\text{d}}, \ \text{subject to} \quad & J\dot{v}_{\text{d}} = p, \ & A\dot{v}_{\text{d}} + \dot{A}v = W_g + Q\rho + \sum_i W_{\text{ext},i}, \ & \rho_{\text{min}} \leq \rho \leq \rho_{\text{max}}, \end{aligned} $$

where (A) is the centroidal momentum matrix, (b = \dot{h_d} - \dot{A}v), (\dot{h_d}) and (\dot{A}v) are the desired and actual rate of change of centroidal momentum, (C_h), (C_\rho), and (C_{\dot{v}}) are cost-function weighting matrices, (J) is a Jacobian matrix that relates the desired joint accelerations (\dot{v_d}) to the motion-task desired accelerations (p), (W_g) is the wrench from gravity, (Q\rho) is the ground-reaction wrench, and (W_{ext,i}) is an external wrench. The resulting ground reaction, pre-specified wrenches, and desired joint accelerations are used as input to an inverse-dynamics solver to compute the desired joint torques (\tau_d).

We use the dynamic walking behavior described in 31, composed of standing, transfer, and swing states with associated motion tasks and active contacts. The controller executes a queue of footstep poses submitted by the behavior layer and uses instantaneous capture point (ICP) 39 to model balance dynamics. Foot pose, pelvis height, and pelvis orientation trajectories are generated to achieve the desired footsteps while maintaining balance. During transfer, an ICP trajectory is generated along with a relaxation of stance-foot contact constraints to allow heel lift. The centroidal moment pivot (CMP) can also be used to exhaust the body’s ability to control balance with momentum. More details can be found in 38.

Foot swings use smoothed trajectories that vary by terrain type. On rough terrain, swings may be planned to avoid tripping; on flat ground, default conservative trajectories with low swing height are used. To recover from external disturbances and instabilities, swings can also be sped up during execution to more quickly catch balance. State estimation combines kinematic foot-ground-contact proprioception for pelvis position with a pelvis-mounted IMU for pelvis orientation. When a foot is in contact (determined by a glitch-filtered, delay-compensated, ankle-torque-sensor-based virtual foot switch), we assume it does not slide, and pelvis position is computed through forward kinematics. The positional estimate is therefore susceptible to drift from inaccurate foot-contact detection, foot slipping, and lack of both feet on the ground.
